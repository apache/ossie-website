# The FSI Semantic Working Group Is Live

AI agents are producing real business outcomes in financial services. The firms
getting there fastest have one thing in common: they solved the semantic layer
first.

The evidence is no longer hypothetical. Asset managers deploying agentic AI are
reporting faster prospect identification and lifts in win rates. Institutional
workflows that previously took days are compressing to minutes. The firms at the
front of this wave are not waiting on better models or faster compute. They are
moving fast because they solved something harder first: they established a
consistent, trusted semantic layer that gives their data meaning before the
agent ever touches it.

<!-- more -->

The firms that haven't solved it yet are stalling at a well-documented barrier.
Industry research identifies it plainly: many organizations struggle to
establish a semantic layer that gives data meaning and business context. Without
it, agents cannot operate with a reliable understanding of the business. The
quality of their outputs — and the decisions made from them — is at risk.

This is not a technology problem. It is a coordination problem. And it is one
that no single institution can solve alone.

## Why This Requires a Collective Response

In financial services, every institution independently models the same core
concepts — trades, positions, instruments, claims — with no shared vocabulary to
anchor them. The structural standard gives us a wire format. It does not give us
a shared language.

The cost is measurable: inefficient integrations, inconsistent AI outputs, and
time-to-value that stretches far beyond what the technology itself requires. And
as AI agents move from experimentation into production — acting autonomously, at
scale, across firm boundaries — the cost of unresolved semantic misalignment
stops being a data quality issue and starts being a business risk.

This is the problem the Financial Services Semantic Working Group was formed to
address.

## The Working Group

Today marks the first formal meeting of the Financial Services Semantic Working
Group, operating under the
[Open Semantic Interchange (OSI)](https://open-semantic-interchange.github.io/osi-website/)
umbrella. This is an industry initiative — convened by practitioners, for
practitioners — with representation from across the full breadth of financial
services.

A few of our current members include Northern Trust, DTCC, BNY, Cotality, LSEG,
Verisk, BlackRock, S&P Global, AIG, Equilar, and TIAA.

Banking. Insurance. Asset management. Wealth management. Market infrastructure.
Data providers. Every major sub-vertical in FSI is at the table, because
semantic alignment that works in only one corner of the industry is not
alignment — it is a slightly larger silo.

## What We Agreed On Today

The first meeting was not about shipping deliverables. It was about agreeing on
the problem, the principles, and the shape of the work ahead.

We ratified a formal problem statement: "The structural standard gives us a wire
format; it does not give us a shared language. In financial services, data
providers and institutions independently model the same core concepts — with no
shared vocabulary to anchor them."

We agreed that FIBO will serve as the default authoritative source for
definitions, with a clear process for cases where FIBO is absent, ambiguous, or
requires FSI-specific extension.

The group aligned on structuring the work by sub-industry — beginning with a
focused set of subdomains, with scope expected to grow as the initiative
matures.

There was strong consensus around the commercial impact: the cost of
misalignment, integration spend, and the time-to-value gap that firms are
absorbing today. Measuring the adoption was seen as essential to sustaining
long-term participation and demonstrating the initiative's value to the broader
industry.

And we agreed that the decisions ahead will not be made in a single meeting.
Building a shared semantic vocabulary for an industry this complex requires a
governance model that is transparent, contestable, and durable — a contribution
workflow that defines who proposes, who reviews, what constitutes approval, and
how disputes are resolved.

## What Comes Next

The working group now moves into parallel workstreams: a literature review of
prior standardisation efforts, and scoping of the first two sub-industry
dictionaries.

The output will not be a framework document. It will be something practitioners
can use — definitions that can be referenced, tooling that makes adoption
practical, and a change process that keeps the vocabulary current as the
industry evolves.

The firms already benefiting from agentic AI got there because someone solved
the semantic layer. We are building the version of that solution that belongs to
the whole industry.

This is early. There is significant work ahead. We are glad to be doing it
together.
